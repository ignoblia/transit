# Legal Rights in Switzerland

## Gender Recognition

Switzerland operates a **medicalized model** for legal gender recognition. A reform bill (self-determination model) has been proposed but as of 2026 has not yet been passed.

### Current Requirements

- Psychiatric diagnosis of gender dysphoria (ICD-11 or DSM-5)
- Court application to change legal gender
- Medical reports from qualified professionals
- No explicit surgery or sterilization requirement (sterilization was removed as a requirement in practice)
- Minimum age: 18

### Process

1. Obtain psychiatric diagnosis (1-3 sessions with a psychiatrist)
2. Collect medical reports supporting the gender change
3. Submit application to the local district court (Bezirksgericht)
4. Court hearing may be required
5. Court decision typically takes 2-6 months

### Pending Reform

A **self-determination bill** has been proposed that would allow gender self-declaration without medical involvement. As of 2026, this reform has not yet passed, but advocacy continues.

## Name Changes

- Name changes can be applied for separately from gender marker change
- Requires application to the civil registry office (Zivilstandsamt)
- Legal gender change usually results in automatic name change
- Name-only changes for trans people require a medical certificate in practice

## Anti-Discrimination Laws

Switzerland has comprehensive but fragmented anti-discrimination protections:

- **Federal Constitution** — Article 8 guarantees equality before the law
- **Criminal Code** — Article 261bis prohibits discrimination based on race, ethnicity, religion, and sexual orientation; gender identity is increasingly recognized
- **Employment Law** — General protections against unfair dismissal
- **Cantonal variations** — Some cantons (Zurich, Geneva, Bern, Basel-Stadt) have stronger protections than others

The **Federal Act on Gender Equality** provides some protections, but gender identity is not explicitly listed in all cantonal laws.

## Hate Crime Laws

- Switzerland's Criminal Code (Article 261bis) covers hate speech and incitement to hatred
- Gender identity is not explicitly named in federal hate crime law
- Some cantons include gender identity in their hate crime provisions
- In practice, prosecution varies significantly by canton
- Advocacy groups are pushing for federal-level inclusion of gender identity

## Document Recognition

- Updated gender markers on Swiss passports and IDs recognized internationally
- Swiss passports allow "X" marker (diverse/divers) since 2022
- Process for "X" marker requires medical documentation
- Swiss ID cards also support updated markers
- Recognized under Swiss and international travel regulations

## Summary

| Aspect | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Gender Recognition | Medicalized (court-based) |
| Self-ID Reform | Proposed, not passed |
| Surgery Required | No (removed in practice) |
| Third Gender | Available ("divers" marker) |
| Anti-Discrimination | Comprehensive but fragmented |
| Hate Crime Protections | Limited (not explicitly trans) |
