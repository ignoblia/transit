# You've Arrived: The First Year

You made it. The planning is done, the flight is over, and you are in your destination country. This is an achievement. Now comes a different kind of work: building a life in a new place while managing your health, your identity, your documents, and your emotional well-being — all at the same time.

This guide covers the concrete steps for the first year and the emotional reality that no guidebook talks about.


## The Emotional Reality of Trans Migration

### What Nobody Tells You

- **Grief is normal.** You left behind people, places, a language, a culture you understood instinctively. Even if you were fleeing danger, you will grieve what you lost. This does not mean you made the wrong choice. It means you are human.

- **Your identity may feel different in a new language.** Pronouns, gendered words, and social expectations around gender vary enormously. In German, every noun has a gender and every interaction requires you to navigate Frau/Herr. In Spanish, adjectives are gendered. In Icelandic, new pronouns (hán, hín) are emerging. You may feel like you are learning to be yourself all over again. This is temporary.

- **Rebuilding community from zero is exhausting.** Making friends as an adult is hard. Making friends as a trans person in a new country where you do not fully speak the language yet is harder. Be patient with yourself. Aim for one or two genuine connections in the first year, not a full social circle.

- **You may not feel safe at first.** Even in a country with strong trans rights, it takes time to internalise that the police might actually help you, that strangers might not harass you, that you can exist in public without scanning for exits. This hypervigilance fades gradually. Give it time.

- **Imposter syndrome is common.** "Do I really belong here? Am I trans enough for this country's healthcare system? Do I deserve this safety?" These thoughts are normal. They are a symptom of the gaslighting you experienced before, not a reflection of reality.


## Healthcare Transfer: Step-by-Step by Country

### Germany

**Process for establishing HRT care:**
1. **Register with a public health insurance provider** (Krankenkasse) — AOK, TK, or Barmer are trans-friendly. You need a German address and anmeldung (residence registration) first.
2. **Find a Hausarzt (GP)** who can manage HRT monitoring. The [Queermed directory](https://www.queermed.de) lists trans-friendly doctors by city.
3. **Get a Indikation (referral letter)** from a therapist. This is the gatekeeping step. You need to find a therapist who will write this after a few sessions — it can take 1–6 months.
4. **Find an endocrinologist** or gynaecologist who prescribes HRT. Wait times are 3–9 months in most cities. Berlin and Cologne have the most options.
5. **Bring:** Your HRT medication, a letter from your previous doctor (translated to German if possible), and your Indikation letter. The endocrinologist will write a new prescription.
6. **Cost:** Public insurance covers HRT (€5–10 co-pay per prescription). Private: €30–60 per month.

**Trans-friendly clinic networks:**
- **Berlin:** Praxis Dr. Mark, Praxis Dr. Bock, Institut für Sexualwissenschaft und Sexualmedizin
- **Cologne:** Praxis Dr. Grüne, Praxis Dr. Rosenthal
- **Hamburg:** Hormonpraxis Dr. Bohnen, Praxis Dr. Kiltz
- **Munich:** Praxis Dr. Loos, Klinikum rechts der Isar

**If you cannot get an appointment quickly:**
- GenderGP (remote prescribing) works in Germany — endocrinologists are not legally required to accept it, but many do
- Your original HRT prescription from abroad can be filled at a German pharmacy for up to 3 months with a foreign prescription (varies by Bundesland)

### Portugal

**Process for establishing HRT care:**
1. **Register with the public healthcare system (SNS).** You need a residence permit and NIF (tax number). Go to your local Centro de Saúde to register.
2. **Request a family doctor (Médico de Família).** Wait times vary from immediate to 6 months depending on the area.
3. **Get a referral to the GAT (Grupo de Ativistas em Tratamentos)** — a trans-led organisation that provides free and low-cost HRT bridging, peer support, and referrals to trans-friendly doctors. GAT is the single most useful resource for trans people in Portugal.
4. **Alternatively:** Find a private endocrinologist (€60–120 per appointment). Many are trans-friendly. Ask in the GAT Facebook group for current recommendations.
5. **Bring:** Your HRT medication and a letter from your previous doctor (Portuguese translation helps).
6. **Cost:** Public SNS: free or minimal co-pay. Private: €30–60/month for HRT.

**Key resources:**
- **GAT Portugal:** [gatportugal.org](https://gatportugal.org) — essential first contact
- **ILGA Portugal:** Legal support and community navigation
- **Transmissão:** Trans-led collective in Lisbon

### Iceland

**Process for establishing HRT care:**
1. **Register with the national healthcare system (Sjúkratryggingar Íslands).** You need a kennitala (Icelandic ID number), which requires residence permission.
2. **Get a referral to the Gender Identity Clinic** at Landspítali University Hospital. This is the only specialist clinic in Iceland and the wait list is long — typically 12–18 months for a first appointment.
3. **In the meantime:** Find a GP (heimilisækni) who will do bridge prescribing. This is uncommon but possible. Ask in the trans community groups (search Facebook for "Trans Ísland" or "Queer Reykjavík").
4. **Alternative:** GenderGP prescribing works from Iceland. Many trans people in Iceland use this while waiting for the public system.
5. **Bring:** Your HRT medication, a detailed letter from your previous doctor (translated to Icelandic if possible), and an Icelandic pharmacy knows your prescription.
6. **Cost:** Public system: minimal co-pay after registration (≈ ISK 1,500–4,000 per appointment). Private: limited options but some private endocrinologists in Reykjavík.

**Important:** Iceland's small population means the trans community is small but tight-knit. Reach out early. People will help you navigate the system.

### Argentina

**Process for establishing HRT care:**
1. **If you have a DNI (Argentine national ID), you are eligible for public healthcare (Sistema Público de Salud).**
2. **Go to a public hospital** with an endocrinology department. Buenos Aires has several trans-friendly hospitals:
   - Hospital Durand
   - Hospital Posadas
   - Hospital Penna
3. **Informed consent is the legal standard** since the Gender Identity Law (2012). You do not need a therapist letter. You show up, say you need HRT, and they will prescribe it after blood tests.
4. **Cost:** Public system: free. Private: very cheap (€10–20/month for HRT).
5. **Language:** Spanish required for public system access. Private endocrinologists who speak English exist in Buenos Aires but cost more.
6. **Trans-specific clinic:** La Casa del Encuentro (Buenos Aires) provides trans healthcare navigation and community support.

**Key law:** Argentina's Gender Identity Law (Law 26.743, 2012) is one of the most progressive in the world. It guarantees:
- Self-declared gender change without surgery, therapy, or court approval
- Full coverage of gender-affirming healthcare in public insurance
- The right to treatment respectful of your gender identity


## Legal Name and Gender Change: Process by Country

### Germany
**Process:**
1. Under the **SBGG** (Selbstbestimmungsgesetz, in force since 1 November 2024), adults can change their name and legal gender by self-declaration at the Standesamt (registry office).
2. **Timeline:** 3 months from application to change. There is a mandatory 3-month waiting period after filing.
3. **Cost:** Approximately €30–60.
4. **Requirements:** You must be 18+. No medical documentation needed. No surgery, therapy, or court order.
5. **Note for non-citizens:** The SBGG applies to anyone legally resident in Germany, not just citizens. You need a valid residence permit and anmeldung.

### Spain
**Process:**
1. Under **Law 3/2023** ("Ley Trans"), self-declaration is legal for anyone 16+ (14–16 with parental consent, 12–14 with judicial approval).
2. **Timeline:** 3–6 months from application. The process is bureaucratic (you file at the Registro Civil).
3. **Cost:** Minimal (administrative fees, varies by region).
4. **Requirements:** No medical documentation. No surgery, therapy, or court order. You simply declare at the registry.
5. **Note for non-citizens:** You must be legally resident in Spain (empadronamiento + residence permit). The law explicitly covers migrants.

### Portugal
**Process:**
1. Under the 2018 Gender Identity Law (Law 38/2018), self-declaration is legal for anyone 18+ (16–18 with parental consent).
2. **Timeline:** 2–6 months. You file at the Conservatória do Registo Civil (Civil Registry).
3. **Cost:** Approximately €200 (registration fees).
4. **Requirements:** No medical documentation needed. No surgery, therapy, or court order.
5. **Note for non-citizens:** You must be legally resident in Portugal.

### Iceland
**Process:**
1. Under the 2019 Gender Autonomy Act, self-declaration is legal for anyone 18+.
2. **Timeline:** 2–4 months. You submit a declaration to the Icelandic National Registry (Þjóðskrá Íslands).
3. **Cost:** Minimal (≈ ISK 5,000–10,000).
4. **Requirements:** No medical documentation. No surgery, therapy, or court order.
5. **Note for non-citizens:** Works for anyone with a kennitala (Icelandic ID number), which requires legal residence.

### Argentina
**Process:**
1. Under the 2012 Gender Identity Law (Law 26.743), self-declaration is legal for any age (minors need judicial approval but the law is progressive).
2. **Timeline:** 1–2 months. You file at the Registro de las Personas.
3. **Cost:** Free.
4. **Requirements:** No medical documentation. No surgery, therapy, or court order. You simply declare and the registry updates your DNI.
5. **Note for non-citizens:** Argentina's law applies to anyone in the country with legal residence. Recent policy changes (2024–2025) have created some uncertainty — check with a local trans rights organisation before relying on this.


## Finding Community Without Prematurely Outing Yourself

Not everyone wants to be publicly out in their new country. Some trans people choose to live stealth. Both paths are valid. Here is how to navigate community building without exposing yourself before you are ready:

### If You Want to Live Stealth
- **Do not join obvious trans groups** on social media connected to your real accounts. Use a separate email and pseudonym for community research.
- **Use private, invite-only platforms.** WhatsApp groups, Signal chats, and small Discord servers are safer than public Facebook groups.
- **Connect with general LGBTQ+ spaces first.** Bars, cafes, and social groups that are "queer-friendly" rather than explicitly trans-focused can help you get oriented without outing yourself.
- **Build professional connections first.** Having a job and professional network gives you a foundation. Community can come later.
- **Consider moving somewhere with enough population density** that you are unlikely to run into people from your community in daily life. Berlin is better than a small German town for this.

### If You Want to Find Trans-Specific Community
- **Identify local trans organisations before you arrive.** For Germany: [TransInterQueer](https://transinterqueer.org) (Berlin), [Trans* in NRW](https://trans.nrw) (Cologne, Düsseldorf), [TransMann](https://transmann.de) (FTM resources). For Spain: [Fundación Triángulo](https://fundaciontriangulo.org), [Cogam](https://cogam.es) (Madrid). For Portugal: [GAT](https://gatportugal.org), [ILGA Portugal](https://ilga-portugal.pt). For Iceland: [Trans Ísland](https://transisland.is), [Queer Reykjavík](https://queer.is).
- **Go to a trans-specific meetup or event** in your first month. It will feel awkward. Go anyway.
- **Use apps like Lex** (text-focused, trans-friendly) or **HER** (for trans women/transfeminine people). Avoid Grindr and Tinder if you are looking for community rather than dates.
- **Language exchange partners** who are also LGBTQ+ can be a low-pressure way to build connections while improving your language skills.


## What to Do If Healthcare Access Is Delayed

Healthcare delays are common in every country for trans-specific care. Have a backup plan.

### Immediate Options (First 2 Weeks)
- **GenderGP:** Remote prescribing from UK-based clinicians. Works in most European countries. Cost: £30/month membership + £15–50 per prescription. The medication is prescribed by a UK pharmacy and shipped to you (if your destination country allows import of personal medication).
- **Bridge clinics:** Some countries have community-led bridging clinics that provide HRT without the full gatekeeping process. Portugal's GAT and Germany's local queer health centres (check your city's list) are examples.
- **Travel to a nearby country** for an initial appointment if your destination country has very long wait times. For example, if you are in Iceland and waitlisted for 12+ months, a weekend trip to Berlin or Amsterdam could get you a prescription.

### Medium-Term Options (1–6 Months)
- **Private healthcare:** In many countries, private endocrinologists have much shorter wait times (weeks rather than months). Research costs before you arrive.
- **DIY HRT:** This option exists. Trans-specific forums can tell you about sources and safety. This guide does not recommend DIY due to quality and safety risks, but acknowledges that some people choose this when all other options fail. Esther/estradiol valerate is safer than injectable estradiol from unreliable sources. Do your research. Get blood tests (you can order them privately in most countries).

### What NOT to Do
- **Do not stop HRT cold turkey** unless medically necessary. If you run out, reduce your dose gradually if possible.
- **Do not accept the first "no" from a doctor.** Find another one. Queermed (Germany), GAT (Portugal), and local trans Facebook groups are better sources for trans-friendly doctors than official healthcare directories.
- **Do not be afraid to switch doctors** if you get gatekept or treated poorly. You are not being demanding — you are managing your healthcare.


## When to Consider Returning vs. Staying: An Honest Framework

Sometimes migration does not work out. This does not mean you failed. It means the circumstances did not align.

**Reasons to stay:**
- You have made progress on language and integration
- You have consistent access to HRT and healthcare
- You feel safer overall, even if some things are hard
- You have at least one genuine connection in your new country
- You have a viable path to permanent residency

**Reasons to consider returning or moving again:**
- Your asylum claim was rejected and appeals are exhausted
- You cannot access HRT or healthcare after 6+ months of trying
- You are experiencing sustained isolation that is harming your mental health
- Your destination country's political situation has changed significantly
- You have a better option in another country

**If you decide to leave:**
- You can apply for asylum or a visa in another country — prior relocation does not bar you
- Returning to your origin country may jeopardise future asylum claims if conditions have not changed
- Consult a lawyer before making any irreversible decision


## Important Notes on the Data Sources

The [Equaldex Equality Index](https://www.equaldex.com/equality-index) is referenced throughout this site. It measures **LGBTQ+ equality broadly** — it includes sexual orientation and gender identity rights but does not specifically measure trans-specific protections. A country with a high Equaldex score does not necessarily have good trans-specific healthcare, gender recognition, or community safety. Wherever possible, this site uses trans-specific sources (TGEU, ILGA trans rights data, community reports) in addition to Equaldex.
