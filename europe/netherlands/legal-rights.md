# Legal Rights in the Netherlands

## Gender Recognition

The Netherlands transitioned to a self-determination model for legal gender recognition in 2025. Previously, the system required a medical expert statement and court approval (since 2014, when the sterilisation requirement was removed). The new law allows individuals aged 16 and over to change their legal gender marker through a simple administrative process at the municipality.

The current process:
- Declaration at the civil registry office (gemeente)
- No medical documentation required
- No surgery or diagnosis requirement
- Minors aged 16-17 can apply with parental consent
- A cooling-off period of several months previously required, now removed

## Name Changes

Name changes follow the gender recognition process. After changing the gender marker, the first name can be updated through the same municipal declaration. For name-only changes (without gender marker change), a separate application to the court is required.

## Anti-Discrimination Laws

The Netherlands has strong anti-discrimination legislation:
- **Equal Treatment Act (Algemene wet gelijke behandeling)** — Prohibits discrimination based on gender, which includes gender identity
- **Employment** — Protection from hiring discrimination to termination
- **Healthcare** — Right to access gender affirming care
- **Education** — Protection in schools and universities
- **Housing and goods/services** — Full protection

The **Netherlands Institute for Human Rights (College voor de Rechten van de Mens)** handles discrimination complaints.

## Hate Crime Laws

The Dutch Penal Code (Wetboek van Strafrecht) includes discrimination as an aggravating circumstance for crimes. Article 137c-137g prohibits:
- Defamation based on protected characteristics
- Incitement to hatred and discrimination
- Violence with discriminatory motives

Gender identity is explicitly covered under these provisions.
