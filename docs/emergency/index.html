I Need to Leave Now ​

This page is for people who do not have months to plan. If you are in immediate physical danger, contact emergency services in your current location first. If you have hours or days rather than months, follow the steps below in priority order.

Immediate Emergency Contacts ​

🆘 Rainbow Railroad — Global Crisis Support ​

  • Website: rainbowrailroad.org
  • Apply for help: rainbowrailroad.org/get-help
  • What they do: Emergency evacuation assistance, relocation support, and direct financial aid for LGBTQ+ people facing persecution. They operate globally and have evacuated individuals from over 40 countries.
  • Response time: Typically 1–7 days for initial assessment.
  • Languages: English, with interpretation available.

🆘 ORAM (Organization for Refuge, Asylum & Migration) ​

  • Website: oramrefugee.org
  • What they do: Legal aid, case management, and direct support for LGBTQ+ asylum seekers and refugees, with a focus on the most vulnerable.
  • Specialisation: Particularly active in Turkey, Lebanon, Jordan, Kenya, and other transit countries.

🆘 Micro Rainbow ​

  • Website: microrainbow.org
  • What they do: Emergency housing, asylum support, and community navigation for LGBTQ+ asylum seekers in the UK and Europe.
  • Services: Emergency accommodation specifically for LGBTQ+ people fleeing persecution.

🆘 UNHCR Emergency Referral ​

  • Website: unhcr.org
  • How it works: If you are in a refugee camp or transit country, UNHCR can issue an emergency referral to a resettlement country. This is a formal process — you need a UNHCR registration number to start.
  • To register: Find your nearest UNHCR office or partner organisation. In many countries, you can self-refer online.

Step 1: What Documents to Grab (Priority Order) ​

If you have 24 hours or less, take only what fits in one bag. Prioritise in this order:

Tier 1 — Essential (take even if you have 10 minutes) ​

  1. Passport (current and expired, if available)
  2. National ID card (if separate from passport)
  3. Birth certificate (original or certified copy)
  4. Mobile phone and charger
  5. Wallet, cash (convert as much as possible to EUR or USD), bank cards

Tier 2 — Safety-Critical (take if you have 1 hour) ​

  1. HRT prescriptions and medication — as much as you can carry
  2. Medical records related to gender-affirming care (clinic letters, surgeon letters, blood test results)
  3. Legal documents proving persecution (police reports, hospital records after an attack, threatening messages, court documents)
  4. Photos of all documents above saved to encrypted cloud storage (use a new account if necessary)

Tier 3 — Important (take if you have 4+ hours) ​

  1. Laptop or tablet (encrypt before leaving)
  2. Proof of LGBTQ+ community affiliation (screenshots of group memberships, event photos, organisational letters — these help prove your claim)
  3. Educational certificates and professional credentials
  4. Family photos and small sentimental items
  5. List of emergency contacts memorised and written on paper

What NOT to take ​

  • Items that could identify others in your community (delete contact lists, group chats, shared photos)
  • Anything you cannot carry for 2 km
  • Unnecessary valuables

Step 2: Transit Risk Assessment ​

Your safest exit route may not be the shortest. A direct flight that passes through a hostile country's airspace or requires a layover there can put you at risk.

How to Check Your Route ​

  1. Check transit visa rules: Some countries require transit visas even for same-day connections. Use timaticweb.com (free via airline booking systems) or the IATA Travel Centre.
  2. Identify hostile transit points: If your only affordable route passes through Russia, Hungary, Poland (some regions), or certain US states, consider taking a longer route.
  3. Avoid connecting through: Countries where your passport gender marker could trigger scrutiny, where anti-LGBTQ+ laws could be used against a trans person in transit, or where border officials have discretion to deny entry.
  4. Aim for direct flights to the safest nearby destination, even if it is not your final target.

If You Must Pass Through a Hostile Country ​

  • Keep documents that show your assigned sex at birth (if it matches your passport marker) easily accessible — you may need to present them.
  • Remove Pride flags, pronouns, or any LGBTQ+-indicative items from your carry-on.
  • Know the generic names of your medications in case you are questioned.
  • Memorise a cover story for why you are travelling (e.g., "visiting family," "medical appointment").
  • Do not mention asylum intentions at immigration — say "tourist" or "transit" until you reach your destination.

Step 3: Digital Safety Before You Go ​

Delete (before crossing any border) ​

  • Messaging app histories (Signal, WhatsApp, Telegram) with LGBTQ+ contacts
  • Browser history, saved passwords, and autofill data
  • Photos that could identify community members
  • Dating app profiles and conversations
  • Cloud storage traces of LGBTQ+ content (if you share a device or account)

Back Up ​

  • Encrypted backup of essential documents to a new email account created on a clean device
  • Contact numbers for your support network — memorise the most important ones
  • HRT prescription details and generic drug names

During Transit ​

  • Use a VPN (ideally one you installed before departure, like Mullvad or ProtonVPN)
  • Enable full-disk encryption on your phone and laptop
  • Keep devices in airplane mode when crossing hostile borders
  • Consider a burner phone for the transit period

Step 5: What to Do in Your First 48 Hours After Arrival ​

  1. Contact Rainbow Railroad or UNHCR at your arrival point — do not wait.
  2. Do not post on social media about your arrival. Do not check in to locations. Do not contact people from your origin country.
  3. Register with local authorities as required (this differs by country — ask your support organisation).
  4. Find LGBTQ+-friendly accommodation — Rainbow Railroad can help arrange this.
  5. Reach out to local trans organisations — they can connect you to community, legal aid, and healthcare faster than official channels.
  6. Replenish HRT medication as soon as possible. Use telehealth services like GenderGP if local healthcare is not immediately accessible.
  7. Report your safe arrival to one trusted person in your origin country (using an encrypted channel).
