# Legal Rights in Poland

## Gender Recognition
Poland has **no comprehensive gender recognition law** — the process relies on civil court decisions that vary dramatically by region:

- **Requirements**: Medical diagnosis of gender dysphoria required
- **Process**: File a lawsuit against parents for "rectification of civil status"
- **Surgery**: Not explicitly required by law, but some judges demand it
- **Government appeals**: The government frequently appeals positive rulings, prolonging the process
- **Age**: 18+ only
- **Regional variation**: Courts in Warsaw and Kraków are more likely to approve; courts in smaller, conservative towns often reject

### The Process
1. Diagnosis from a sexuologist or psychiatrist
2. Minimum period of documented HRT and real-life experience
3. File a civil lawsuit against parents to change gender marker
4. Attend court hearings (typically 2-4 over 6-18 months)
5. If approved, update civil registry and obtain new documents

**Note**: The European Court of Human Rights ruled in 2024 that Poland violates human rights by failing to provide clear legal gender recognition procedures. The process remains humiliating, expensive, and uncertain.

## Name Changes
- Processed through the **Civil Registry Office** (Urząd Stanu Cywilnego)
- Name change can be done before or separately from gender marker change
- Requires a valid reason
- Processed within 1-2 months typically
- Some registrars may refuse — appeals are possible

## Anti-Discrimination Laws
- **Employment**: Prohibited under the Labour Code for sexual orientation, but gender identity is not explicitly mentioned
- **Goods and Services**: General protections under the Act on Implementation of Certain EU Regulations
- **Enforcement**: The Commissioner for Human Rights (Rzecznik Praw Obywatelskich) handles complaints
- Practical enforcement is weak, especially outside major cities

### "LGBTQ+ Free Zones"
- Over 100 municipalities have adopted resolutions declaring themselves "free from LGBTQ+ ideology"
- These are symbolic but signal social hostility
- Some municipalities have rescinded these resolutions after EU funding pressure

## Hate Crime Laws
- The **Criminal Code** includes hate crime provisions for race, religion, nationality
- Sexual orientation is included, but enforcement is inconsistent
- Gender identity is **not explicitly mentioned**
- Far-right groups are active and attacks on LGBTQ+ people occur
- Police response varies significantly by region

## Document Recognition
- Updated passports and ID cards are recognized internationally
- Previous documents are archived but not publicly accessible
- People with changed documents may still face issues with certain databases remaining unchanged
- The gender marker mismatch between documents and presentation can cause problems
