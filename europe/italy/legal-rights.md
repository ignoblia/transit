# Legal Rights in Italy

## Gender Recognition
Italy's **Law 164/1982** was one of the first in Europe to allow legal gender recognition, but the process remains heavily medicalized and judicial:

- **Requirements**: Diagnosis of gender dysphoria + undergoing HRT or surgery
- **Court Authorization**: A court order (from the Tribunale) is required to change legal gender
- **Surgery**: Historically required; recent case law (Constitutional Court rulings 2015, 2017) clarified that surgery is not mandatory, though many judges still expect it
- **Age**: 18+ can apply; minors require court authorization
- **Process**: File a petition with the civil court; medical expert report required; court hearing; final decree

### The Judicial Process
1. Obtain a diagnosis from a recognized gender clinic or mental health professional
2. Undergo HRT (typically 1-2 years documented)
3. File a petition with the civil court in your province
4. Court appoints an independent expert (CTU) for evaluation
5. Court hearing and decision
6. Update civil records and IDs

**Total time**: 12-24 months typically

## Name Changes
- Name change is handled as part of the gender recognition court process
- Can also be done separately through the civil court for name only
- Requires the same medical documentation
- Processed concurrently with gender marker change

## Anti-Discrimination Laws
- **Employment**: Protection under the Jobs Act (2015) and EU directives
- **Housing**: General anti-discrimination provisions apply
- **Healthcare**: Access to trans healthcare is a recognized right
- **Public Services**: Protections under consolidated anti-discrimination laws

Enforcement varies significantly by region — northern Italy generally has better implementation than southern regions.

## Hate Crime Laws
- Italy does not have explicit hate crime legislation covering gender identity in all regions
- Some regions have local laws against discrimination based on gender identity
- The **Mancino Law** (1993) covers race, ethnicity, and religion but not gender identity
- A bill to add gender identity and sexual orientation to hate crime laws has been proposed multiple times but not passed
- **Milan, Turin, Bologna, and Florence** have local ordinances against transphobia

## Document Recognition
- Updated passports and IDs are recognized internationally
- Italian ID cards and passports reflect the updated gender marker
- Previous records can be accessed only with legal authorization
- EU mutual recognition applies for travel within the Schengen Area
