# Legal Rights in Portugal

## Gender Recognition

Portugal adopted a self-determination model for legal gender recognition through **Law 38/2018** (Gender Identity Law), passed in July 2018. The law allows individuals aged 18 and over to change their legal gender and name on official documents through a simple administrative process.

The process:
- Application at the **Civil Registry Office (Conservatória do Registo Civil)**
- No medical reports, surgery, or psychological assessments required
- No requirement for divorce or marital status change
- Completed within approximately 30-60 days
- For minors aged 16-18, parental consent required
- Minors under 16 require judicial authorisation

The law explicitly prohibits any requirement for sterilisation, medical treatment, or diagnosis.

## Name Changes

Name changes are processed as part of the gender recognition application at the Civil Registry Office. A new first name consistent with the affirmed gender can be chosen. For name changes without gender marker change, a separate administrative process exists through the **Institute of Registries and Notaries (IRN)**.

## Anti-Discrimination Laws

Portugal prohibits discrimination based on gender identity and sexual orientation:
- **Constitutional protection** — Article 13 of the Portuguese Constitution prohibits discrimination
- **Labour Code** — Prohibits discrimination in employment
- **Law 38/2018** — Explicitly prohibits discrimination against transgender people
- **Conversion therapy** — Banned in 2023
- **Healthcare** — Right to access gender affirming care without discrimination
- **Education** — Protection in schools
- **Housing** — Anti-discrimination in rental and purchase

The **Commission for Equality and Against Racial Discrimination (CICDR)** handles complaints.

## Hate Crime Laws

Portugal's Penal Code includes sexual orientation and gender identity as aggravating circumstances for crimes. The law provides for enhanced penalties when crimes are motivated by hatred or prejudice. This covers:
- Physical violence and assault
- Threats and intimidation
- Property damage
- Hate speech

However, enforcement and reporting mechanisms are still developing compared to Nordic countries.
