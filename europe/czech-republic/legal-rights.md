# Legal Rights in the Czech Republic

## Gender Recognition

The Czech Republic operates one of the most restrictive **medicalized models** in the EU. Legal gender recognition requires **sterilization**, which has been widely condemned by human rights organizations.

### Requirements

- Psychiatric diagnosis of gender dysphoria
- **Sterilization** (removal of reproductive organs — hysterectomy, oophorectomy, or orchiectomy) or long-term hormone therapy
- Court application
- Name change through the registry
- Psychological evaluation
- Minimum age: 18

### Process

1. Psychiatric assessment at the **Sexuologický ústav** (Institute of Sexology) in Prague or other authorized clinics
2. Diagnosis of gender dysphoria
3. Hormone therapy for a period of time
4. Sterilization surgery or evidence of long-term hormone treatment
5. Court application for legal gender change
6. Registration with the civil registry

### Key Issues

- **Sterilization requirement** — The Czech Republic is one of the few EU countries that still requires sterilization for legal gender recognition
- The European Court of Human Rights has ruled against such requirements (e.g., Y.Y. v. Turkey)
- Reform was attempted but stalled in parliament
- The Constitutional Court has been petitioned multiple times
- As of 2026, no reform has been enacted

### Attempted Reform

- A bill to remove the sterilization requirement was proposed in 2021-2022
- It passed initial readings but stalled due to political opposition
- The government has expressed willingness to reform but no timeline
- Legal challenges continue through domestic and European courts

## Name Changes

- Name changes are linked to the legal gender recognition process
- Can apply for a name change separately, but the process is more difficult
- Requires medical documentation supporting a different name
- Processed through the civil registry (Matrika)
- Takes approximately 1-3 months

## Anti-Discrimination Laws

The Czech Republic has moderate anti-discrimination laws, shaped by EU membership:

- **Anti-Discrimination Act (2009)** — Prohibits discrimination in employment, education, social security, and access to goods and services
- **Gender identity** is not explicitly named in the act
- Courts have interpreted "sex" discrimination to include gender identity in some cases
- **Employment** — Protection in hiring, promotion, and dismissal
- **Education** — Protection in schools and universities
- **Healthcare** — Right to access care without discrimination

## Hate Crime Laws

- The Criminal Code includes hate crime provisions with enhanced penalties
- Gender identity is not explicitly listed as a protected characteristic
- Sexual orientation is covered; gender identity coverage is inconsistent
- Reporting of transphobic hate crimes is low
- Far-right groups have been increasingly vocal, particularly online
- Prague police have specialized hate crime units

## Document Recognition

- Updated gender markers on Czech passports and IDs are recognized
- EU standard documents
- Third gender / "X" marker not available
- Birth certificate update requires the court order
- Process is slow and bureaucratic

## Summary

| Aspect | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Gender Recognition | Medicalized (sterilization required) |
| Surgery Required | Yes (sterilization) |
| Third Gender | Not available |
| Anti-Discrimination | Moderate (gender identity not explicit) |
| Hate Crime Protections | Limited |
