Fleeing, Not Moving: The Asylum Pathway ​

This page is for people who cannot safely remain in their country of origin and need international protection. Asylum is not migration — it is a legal process for people fleeing persecution. The two paths look similar but have very different timelines, evidence requirements, and legal frameworks.

If you are in immediate danger, go to the Emergency page first.

Asylum vs. Voluntary Migration: Know Which Path You Are On ​

FactorAsylumVoluntary Migration
Why you leavePersecution, fear for your safetyBetter opportunities, quality of life
Legal framework1951 Refugee Convention, national asylum lawImmigration law, visa systems
Evidence neededProof of persecution (police reports, threats, medical records)Financial means, skills, education
Timeline1–5 years for full process (varies hugely by country)1–24 months depending on visa type
Can you work?Usually after 3–12 months (varies by country)Depends on visa conditions
Can you return home?Not without jeopardising your claimYes, freely
Path to citizenshipYes, after refugee status + residencyYes, through normal naturalisation

Who the asylum path is for: People who face country-wide persecution based on their trans identity — not just occasional harassment, but a well-founded fear of serious harm that their home government cannot or will not protect them from.

Who the asylum path is NOT for: People who are safe in their home country but want better legal recognition, healthcare, or quality of life abroad. If you can safely return home, you are not a refugee under international law.

Countries with the Strongest LGBTQ+ Asylum Grant Rates ​

Grant rates fluctuate year to year and depend on the applicant's country of origin. The data below is based on publicly available government statistics and reports from refugee advocacy organisations.

Tier 1: High Grant Rates (60–90%) ​

Canada ​

  • Estimated grant rate: 70–85% for LGBTQ+ claims
  • Legal basis: Gender identity and sexual orientation recognised as protected grounds for asylum (IRB guidelines since 1993, updated 2021)
  • Process time: 12–24 months for initial hearing (IRB processing times vary)
  • Work permit: Available after 8–12 months (though processing delays mean it often takes longer, but legal entitlement exists)
  • Healthcare: Interim Federal Health Program covers HRT and gender-affirming care for resettled refugees
  • Key case law: Re: X (Re) [1995] established that trans identity can form the basis of a refugee claim; Canada v. Ward [1993] set key refugee definition standards
  • Note: Canada has both a resettlement program (for people referred by UNHCR from outside Canada) and an inland asylum system (for people who arrive and claim at a port of entry or from inside Canada)

Germany ​

  • Estimated grant rate: 60–80% for LGBTQ+ claims (varies by origin country and Bundesland)
  • Legal basis: Section 3 Asylum Act recognises persecution based on sexual orientation and gender identity; 2012 Federal Office for Migration (BAMF) guidelines
  • Process time: 6–18 months for initial decision; appeals can take 2+ years
  • Work permit: Available after 3 months (with some conditions)
  • Healthcare: Standard public healthcare covers HRT after registration; gender-affirming surgeries may be covered with therapist approval
  • Regional variation: Berlin, Hamburg, and NRW have the most supportive asylum courts; Bavaria and Saxony are stricter
  • Worth knowing: Germany's asylum system is overloaded. Processing times are unpredictable. If you can get a visa through other means (study, work), that path is often faster.

Sweden ​

  • Estimated grant rate: 65–80% for LGBTQ+ claims
  • Legal basis: Swedish Migration Agency recognises gender identity and sexual orientation as protected grounds; UNHCR Handbook guidance applied
  • Process time: 12–24 months
  • Work permit: Available during the process for some applicants (LMA card allows work if the Migration Agency determines the claim has "substantive grounds")
  • Note: Sweden interprets "internal flight alternative" more broadly than some countries — you may be asked why you cannot relocate within your own country

Netherlands ​

  • Estimated grant rate: 60–75% for LGBTQ+ claims
  • Legal basis: Aliens Circular 2000 (amended) recognises gender identity persecution
  • Process time: 6–18 months (one of the faster EU systems)
  • Work permit: Available after 6 months on the labour market (varies)
  • Key strength: COA (Central Agency for the Reception of Asylum Seekers) provides accommodation, meals, and healthcare during the process
  • Note: The Netherlands uses country-of-origin specific policies — some nationalities face much lower grant rates

Finland ​

  • Estimated grant rate: 55–75% for LGBTQ+ claims
  • Legal basis: Finnish Aliens Act specifically recognises persecution based on sexual orientation and gender identity
  • Process time: 6–12 months for initial decision (relatively fast)
  • Work permit: Available after 3 months (with restrictions)
  • Note: Finland's grant rates for trans-specific claims have been improving, but documentation requirements are strict

Tier 2: Moderate Grant Rates (40–60%) ​

  • United Kingdom: 50–65% (but long delays; Home Office hostile environment policy affects some claims; 2021 Nationality and Borders Act introduced "differentiation" for late claims)
  • Ireland: 50–70% (smaller system; processing can be slow; commitment to temporary protection strong)
  • Belgium: 50–65% (CGRS has specific guidance for LGBTQ+ claims; relatively fast process)
  • France: 45–60% (OFPRA has LGBTQ+ unit; process can be slow for some nationalities; appeals averaged 12–18 months)
  • United States: 30–50% (varies enormously by immigration court circuit; highly politicised; some circuits are far more favourable than others; current administration policy is rapidly evolving)

⚠️ Important

Asylum grant rates change with government policy, court appointments, and geopolitical shifts. The data above reflects 2023–2025 trends. Always verify current rates with a qualified immigration lawyer or refugee support organisation before making decisions.

What Evidence You Need to Build a Credible Asylum Claim ​

Your claim needs to establish three things: (1) you are transgender, (2) you face persecution because of it, and (3) your government cannot or will not protect you.

Evidence of Your Trans Identity ​

  • Medical records showing gender-affirming care (HRT prescriptions, surgery letters, therapy notes)
  • Letters from healthcare providers confirming your treatment history
  • Affidavits from friends, family, or community members who know your identity
  • Photos before and after transition (if safe to store)
  • Identity documents from your home country that show your gender marker (even if outdated)

Evidence of Persecution ​

  • Police reports documenting attacks, harassment, or refused protection
  • Medical records of injuries from attacks (hospital admissions, doctor's notes)
  • Threat letters, messages, or social media posts targeting you
  • Court documents if you were charged under anti-LGBTQ+ laws
  • News articles documenting anti-trans violence or legal changes in your country
  • Watchdog reports from Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, ILGA, or TGEU documenting the situation for trans people in your country
  • Witness statements from people who saw the persecution

Evidence of Country Conditions ​

  • Country of origin reports (UK Home Office Country Policy Notes, US State Department Human Rights Reports)
  • ILGA State-Sponsored Homophobia report (annual, maps anti-LGBTQ+ laws globally)
  • TGEU Trans Rights Map (shows legal gender recognition, healthcare access)
  • UNHCR guidance notes on your country of origin
  • Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International reports

Evidence of Community Affiliation ​

  • Proof of membership in LGBTQ+ organisations or groups
  • Event attendance records (Pride, community meetings)
  • Social media activity showing your involvement (screenshots, printouts)
  • Letters of support from organisations you worked with

How to Respond to "Why Didn't You Just Be Discreet?" ​

This is one of the most common reasons asylum claims are rejected. The logic is: if you hide your identity, you won't be persecuted, so you don't need protection.

This is wrong under international law, but that does not mean it will not be used against you. Here is how to address it:

The Legal Answer ​

The UN Refugee Agency (UNHCR) issued UNHCR Guidelines on International Protection No. 9 (2012) which explicitly states:

"It is not appropriate for decision-makers to expect a claimant to conceal their sexual orientation or gender identity in order to avoid persecution."

In UNHCR's 2008 guidance specifically on LGBTQ+ claims, it says: "requiring an individual to be 'discreet' would be tantamount to requiring them to renounce a fundamental characteristic."

The UN Human Rights Committee has affirmed that "the right to be oneself" is a fundamental human right, and the European Court of Human Rights in M.E. v. Sweden (2014) found that requiring discretion violates refugee protection principles.

Practical Preparation ​

Despite the law, some case officers will still ask this question. Be prepared to explain:

  • That being "discreet" means living in constant fear — which is itself persecution
  • That even if you tried to hide who you are, the risk of discovery remains (landlords, employers, medical providers, extended family)
  • That concealment requires you to suppress a core aspect of your identity, which has severe psychological consequences
  • That the very need to hide proves your well-founded fear
  • That in many countries, authorities find out anyway through medical records, ID checks, or informants — you cannot control all routes of discovery

Documentation strategy: Include a statement in your affidavit addressing this explicitly. Write it yourself in your own words, not copied from a manual. Case officers can tell.

UNHCR's Specific Guidance for LGBTQ+ Asylum Seekers ​

The UNHCR has issued several guidance documents that are directly relevant:

  1. UNHCR Guidelines on International Protection No. 9 (2012): Sexual orientation and/or gender identity refugee claims.

    • unhcr.org
    • Key principle: Refusing to hide identity is not a basis for rejecting a claim.

  2. UNHCR Guidance Note on Refugee Claims Relating to Sexual Orientation and Gender Identity (2008):

    • First comprehensive guidance on trans-specific claims
    • Established that surgical transition is not required to be considered trans

  3. UNHCR Resettlement Handbook:

    • Outlines resettlement criteria for people who cannot return home
    • LGBTQ+ people from countries with anti-LGBTQ+ laws are explicitly recognised as a priority group

How to Get a UNHCR Referral ​

If you are outside your country of origin but not yet in a safe third country:

  1. Register with UNHCR in your current country of residence (find your nearest office via the UNHCR website)
  2. Attend registration and eligibility interview
  3. If determined to be a refugee (Refugee Status Determination), you become eligible for resettlement
  4. UNHCR refers cases to resettlement countries (Canada, Germany, Sweden, Norway, US, UK are the largest recipients)
  5. The process takes 1–5 years from registration to resettlement, depending on the country and case urgency

What to Do If You Have Days Rather Than Months ​

  1. Contact Rainbow Railroad immediately — do not wait. Their entire purpose is to help people in exactly this situation.
  2. Pack only one bag — documents, medication, charging cables, one change of clothes.
  3. Get to the airport or border — if you can, reach a country that accepts asylum claims at the border (or at a port of entry).
  4. Claim asylum at the border — when you arrive at immigration, say: "I am transgender and I am fleeing persecution. I want to apply for asylum." In many countries, including Canada, Germany, and Sweden, border officials are required to register your claim.
  5. Do not destroy your documents — even if they have your deadname or wrong gender marker, they are evidence.
  6. Delete digital traces — go through the digital safety checklist on the Emergency page.
  7. Memorise three phone numbers — Rainbow Railroad, a contact in your destination country, and a lawyer if you have one.

Important Notes on the Data Sources ​

The Equaldex Equality Index is referenced throughout this site. It measures LGBTQ+ equality broadly — it includes sexual orientation and gender identity rights but does not specifically measure trans-specific protections. A country with a high Equaldex score does not necessarily have good trans-specific healthcare, gender recognition, or community safety. Wherever possible, this site uses trans-specific sources (TGEU, ILGA trans rights data, community reports) in addition to Equaldex.
