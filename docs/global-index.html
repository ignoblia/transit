Global Index & Comparison Matrix ​

This matrix compares 25 countries across five key dimensions for transgender individuals considering relocation. Each dimension is scored 1–5 (5 being best). The Equaldex Equality Index score is provided for reference — note that it measures LGBTQ+ equality broadly and is not trans-specific.

⚠️ Important

The Equaldex Equality Index measures LGBTQ+ equality broadly, including same-sex marriage, adoption rights, and sexual orientation laws. A high Equaldex score does not necessarily mean a country has good trans-specific healthcare, gender recognition, or community safety. This matrix provides trans-specific scoring to address that gap.

Scoring Methodology ​

Legal (1–5) ​

Based on: gender recognition model (self-ID vs. medicalised vs. banned), anti-discrimination protections, hate crime laws covering gender identity, same-sex marriage/adoption rights.

  • 5 = Self-ID legal, comprehensive anti-discrimination laws, hate crime laws include gender identity
  • 4 = Self-ID or medicalised with easy process, strong protections
  • 3 = Medicalised with barriers, partial protections
  • 2 = Difficult or no recognition, weak protections
  • 1 = Banned recognition, no protections, active criminalisation

Healthcare (1–5) ​

Based on: HRT access model (informed consent vs. gatekeeping), public coverage of gender-affirming care, wait times, availability of trans-friendly providers.

  • 5 = Informed consent available, public healthcare covers HRT and surgeries, wait times under 3 months
  • 4 = Informed consent in major cities, public coverage of HRT, wait times 3–6 months
  • 3 = Medicalised gatekeeping, public coverage partial, wait times 6–12 months
  • 2 = Medicalised with strict requirements, limited coverage, wait times over 12 months
  • 1 = No access, banned or criminalised

Safety (1–5) ​

Based on: hate crime rates against trans people, police responsiveness, social acceptance, legal protections, regional variation.

  • 5 = Very low risk, strong police protection, high social acceptance nationwide
  • 4 = Low risk in most areas, good police response, social acceptance moderate
  • 3 = Moderate risk, mixed police response, acceptance varies by region
  • 2 = High risk in many areas, limited police protection, significant discrimination
  • 1 = Active danger, state-sponsored persecution, no effective protection

Cost (1=Expensive, 5=Affordable) ​

Based on: typical monthly budget for a single person (rent, food, healthcare, transport, utilities) in the main city.

  • 5 = Under €800/month
  • 4 = €800–1,500/month
  • 3 = €1,500–2,500/month
  • 2 = €2,500–3,500/month
  • 1 = Over €3,500/month

Visa Access (1–5) ​

Based on: availability of digital nomad/remote work visas, ease of work visa sponsorship, processing times, path to permanent residency, barriers for non-EU citizens.

  • 5 = Open digital nomad visa, multiple visa pathways, clear path to PR
  • 4 = Digital nomad visa available, moderate processing, PR possible within 5 years
  • 3 = Limited visa options, moderate barriers, PR path exists but slow
  • 2 = Few visa options, high barriers, long path to PR
  • 1 = Extreme barriers, no realistic path to long-term residence

Key to Legal Gender Recognition Models ​

  • Self-ID (Self-Declaration): Legal gender can be changed through a simple administrative process without requiring medical intervention, surgery, or court approval. Gold standard.
  • Medicalised: Legal gender change requires psychiatric diagnosis, hormone therapy, surgery, or court approval. Creates barriers.
  • Banned: Legal gender recognition is not legally possible regardless of medical steps.

Key to Healthcare Access Models ​

  • Informed Consent: HRT can be prescribed after a discussion of risks and benefits, without requiring a therapist's letter or psychiatric evaluation. Lower barrier.
  • Gatekeeping: HRT requires therapist referral, psychiatric evaluation, or approval from a specialised committee. Higher barrier, longer wait times.
  • Public Coverage: Gender-affirming care is covered by the public health insurance system (may have co-pays).
  • Private Only: Gender-affirming care is only available through private insurance or out-of-pocket payment.
